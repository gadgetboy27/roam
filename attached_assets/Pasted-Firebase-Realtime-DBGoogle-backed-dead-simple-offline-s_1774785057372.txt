Option 3 — Native device SMS (what you were hinting at)
Technically possible via Twilio — you pay per message (~NZD $0.08/SMS) and messages land in the user's native Messages app. Zero app required.
Why not to do this: Every major app deliberately avoids it. The moment users swap numbers the app loses them — no engagement data, no safety tools, no revenue. Bumble, Hinge and Tinder all actively prevent number-sharing in early conversations for exactly this reason. Keep them in the app.

What the Big Apps Actually Built
Tinder uses Node.js backend with MongoDB and Redis, WebSockets for real-time chat, and AWS hosting. Bumble uses Node.js, DynamoDB and Redis. Hinge uses Python/Django with PostgreSQL and AWS. NoGood All three built their own WebSocket infrastructure once they scaled past early tooling.
WebSockets are the standard for real-time messaging and push notifications in dating apps — they keep a persistent connection open so messages arrive instantly without the app needing to poll. Substack

The ROAM-specific messaging feature nobody else has
The Adventure Opener. When a match is made, Claude generates one auto-suggested opener based on the shared tags and Almost Met data. It appears as a tap-to-send chip above the keyboard:

The user taps it, it sends. One tap. The awkward blank screen problem is solved. This is doable in a single Claude API call at match time and costs fractions of a cent per match. No other app does this with geographic specificity — it's uniquely ROAM.
